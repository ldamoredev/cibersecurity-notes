DNS Resolution

Definition

DNS resolution is the process of turning a hostname into the records needed to reach a destination, such as A, AAAA, CNAME, MX, or TXT records. It is the practical lookup path that connects naming to network reachability.

Why it matters

Security work often depends not just on DNS records existing, but on understanding how names resolve through chains of records and providers. Resolution affects: - asset discovery - ownership assumptions - staging and forgotten environments - third-party dependencies - takeover risk through dangling CNAMEs

This note focuses on how names resolve in practice. dns-security covers DNS as an attack-surface and ownership problem, while subdomain-takeover covers one concrete exploit path that falls out of bad DNS hygiene.

Attacker perspective

Attackers use DNS resolution to: - discover how a hostname is actually mapped - identify external providers in the resolution chain - detect stale CNAMEs pointing to removed infrastructure - infer relationships between apps, environments, and vendors

Defender perspective

Defenders should understand: - how authoritative answers are produced - which names depend on third parties - whether CNAME chains point to decommissioned resources - which records expose unnecessary information about the environment

Practical examples

  • app.example.com resolves through a CNAME to a cloud service that no longer exists.
  • Internal naming patterns become obvious from public DNS records.
  • A CDN or load balancer sits in the resolution path, masking the origin architecture.

Useful commands

# Trace record resolution
dig app.example.com
dig app.example.com CNAME
dig +short app.example.com

# Inspect full answer sections
dig app.example.com ANY
